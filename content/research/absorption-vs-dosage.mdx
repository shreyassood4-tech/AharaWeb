The supplement aisle competes on numbers — 1000µg B12, 5000 IU Vitamin D, "ultra-strength." The message: more is better.

The problem: getting a vitamin into a capsule is the easy part. Getting it from the capsule into your bloodstream is where most supplements fail quietly.

## What Absorption Actually Means

A nutrient doesn't travel directly from supplement to cell. It needs to:

1. Survive stomach acid without degrading
2. Reach the right section of the digestive tract
3. Find the right chemical environment — co-factors, pH, gut bacteria
4. Cross from the gut into the bloodstream
5. Travel to the target tissue in a usable form

Each step can fail. And most supplement formulations don't account for any of them.

## The Co-Factor Problem

D3 is fat-soluble — without dietary fat present in the gut when you take it, absorption is negligible. This is why studies comparing D3 taken with and without food show dramatically different outcomes. A supplement listing 2000 IU might deliver 200 IU if taken on an empty stomach.

B12 needs "intrinsic factor" — a protein produced in the stomach — to be absorbed in the small intestine. It also depends on a healthy gut microbiome to support that uptake. Disrupt that environment through stress, antibiotics, or age, and absorption suffers regardless of the dose on the label.

## Why Dosage Numbers Mislead

A supplement listing 500µg B12 isn't lying — that's what's in the capsule. But if the formulation ignores absorption conditions, only a fraction reaches circulation. This is the standard explanation for why people taking daily supplements still show up deficient on blood tests.

"Bioavailability" — the proportion of a nutrient that enters circulation — varies enormously based on formulation, not just dose. It's the number the label never shows you.
